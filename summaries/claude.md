# Claude Summary

NASA JPL researchers have confirmed that a near-Earth object once classified as asteroid 1998 SH2 is actually a comet. In August 2025, the object failed to appear where predicted during a close pass within 2 million miles of Earth, alerting scientists that unmodeled forces were affecting its motion. Using optical astrometry, study lead Davide Farnocchia found nongravitational perturbations inconsistent with an asteroid, pointing instead to gas venting from a comet. Observations from the Canada-France-Hawaii Telescope, the Very Large Telescope, and the Danish Telescope revealed a faint tail, confirming the comet hypothesis, according to coauthor Olivier Hainaut. The object has been redesignated P/1998 SH2. Published in Nature Astronomy, the findings also inform research on "dark comets" — objects with irregular orbits but no visible tail or coma, about a dozen of which have been found since 2016. Farnocchia notes that tracking such perturbations helps distinguish comets from asteroids and better predict Earth-impact risk for planetary defense.
