New research led by scientists at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory in Southern California identified the near-Earth object 1998 SH2 as a comet after precisely tracking its motion and observing a weak but clear tail. Published in Nature Astronomy, the study followed the object's safe passage within 2 million miles (3 million kilometers) of Earth on Aug. 28, 2025, during its 4½-year orbit around the Sun. Researchers found nongravitational perturbations caused by gas venting as the Sun heated ice mixed with rocky material. Davide Farnocchia proposed it was an active comet, and observations from the Canada-France-Hawaii Telescope, the European Southern Observatory's Danish Telescope, and the Very Large Telescope confirmed this, leading to the designation P/1998 SH2. The study also highlights dark comets, notes about a dozen have been identified since 2016, and says NASA's upcoming Near-Earth Object (NEO) Surveyor will help detect faint near-Earth objects.
